Jackson Pollock

biographyartist

Jackson Pollock by Martha Holmes - Photograph (1949)

American abstract expressionism painter from Wyoming (1912 - 1956)

  • Known as the inventor of the Drip Painting style and contributor to the abstract expressionism movement.
    • He draw attention to other artists such as Kooning, Gorky, Motherwell, Newman, and Rothko.
  • Fifth and youngest son of Stella May McClure and LeRoy Pollock, he moved with his family multiple times.
  • By 1930 he followed his brother Charles to NY to study art where he enrolled at the Art Students League.
    • Once in school he studied under regionalist painter Thomas Hart Benton, until he dropped in 1933.
    • For the next years he lived in poverty until employed by the WPA Federal Art Project as an easel painter.
    • While he continued improving his art he also became volatile, emotionally unstable and an alcoholic.
  • In 1937 he began psychiatric treatment for his alcoholism, suffering a serious nervous breakdown in 1938.
  • In 1939 he attended a Picasso exhibition that led Pollock to recognize the power of European expressive modernism.
    • He was also influenced by Diego Rivera, Joan Mir√≥, Jos√© Orozco, Jungian symbolism and the surrealist movement.
    • He became one of the founders of the abstract expressionism that grew in popularity in NY after WWII.
    • He wanted to represent the human irrationality and vulnerability that he had experienced from war and modern life.
  • In 1943 the Federal Art Project ended and Pollock was given a contract by Peggy Guggenheim to sponsor his art.
    • He married Lee Krasner, moved to Long Island in 1945 and started to develop his Drip Painting style in 1947.
  • His exhibitions were later handled by Betty Parsons Gallery from 1947 to 1952 and finally by the Sidney Janis Gallery.
  • To disuade people from a pre-conceived impression of what they where looking at he started to number his paintings.
  • From 1953 his paintings started to became opaque, a reflection of his deteriorating mental health.
    • He died in 1956 at the age of 44 from an alcohol related car accident.

Moby Dick - Gouache and Ink (1943)

Based on the sixth book by Herman Melville, it has been questioned if Pollock actually read the novel however both authors touch on the themes of paranoia and a preoccupation with tragic violence.

Guardians of the Secret - Oil (1943)

Representation of the feeling in Indian rituals that Pollock witnessed as a child, some believe that he tried to adopt the spiritual significance of Native American ritual painting when developing his Drip Painting style.

Male and Female - Oil (1943)

Included in one of his first solo exhibitions run by Peggy Guggenheim, the painting depicts a male figure in black with and a curvy female figure to the left.

There Were Seven in Eight - Oil and Enamel (1945)

This several month painting uses the figurative imagery of heads, eyes, bodies, snakes and fantastic creatures under a network of black lines that prevent any focal point from emerging, marking his transition into nonrepresentational works.

Shimmering Substance - Oil (1946)

One of his first completely nonrepresentational works, he squeezed the paint directly into the canvas and manipulated it with a palette knife or his fingers to create open loops. There is an allusion to light and nature, possibly a sunny summer day.

Eyes in the Heat - Oil and Enamel (1946)

The light, flora and fauna of Long Island are evoked in the painting, this painting shows a thick and textured crust, it's possible to identify the eyes of some creatures concealed by the paint.

Full Fathom Five - Oil (1947)

One of the first drip paintings, it has particularly strange layer of oil paint combined with nails, buttons, keys, coins, cigarettes, matches and other items. The title was suggested by a neighbor from a line from Shakespeare's play The Tempest.

A reviewer a while back wrote that my pictures didn't have any beginning or any end. He didn't mean it as a compliment, but it was. It was a fine compliment. Only he didn't know it.

War - Ink and Pencil (1947)

His only drawing ever titled, created in 1947 but related to his earlier works. A fierce depiction of the destruction caused by WWII, represented by a tortured human body, a bull and various human parts.

Lucifer - Oil and Enamel (1947)

Modern painting that started his use of black enamel and aluminum paints, traditionally reserved for industrial uses. The green was added by putting the canvas on its side for a final set of horizontal drips. Pollock gave the painting to Grant Mark, a chemist that tried to help him with his alcoholism, the title refers both to the morning star and to the fallen angel.

No. 17A - Oil on Fiberboard (1948)

Owned by Hedge fund manager Kenneth C. Griffin, purchased in 2015 for $200 million, a then record breaking price. The piece was featured on the 1949 edition of Life that made Pollock a celebrity.

Arabesque No. 13A - Oil and Enamel (1948)

Pollock is said to have liked it enough that this was the painting he choose to display at his living room.

Summertime No. 9A - Oil and Enamel (1948)

Another abstract expressionism attempt at representing the inner world.

No. 10 - Oil (1949)

Unlike other drip paintings this one includes random trails of black enamel signaling his emotional struggle with alcoholism.

No. 30 - Enamel (1950)

Also known as Autumn Rhythm, its size envelopes the viewer, a display of subconscious creativity and choreography.

One No. 31 - Oil (1950)

Masterpiece of Jackson Pollock, one of the largest and most iconic drip style paintings, the other two being No. 30 and Blue Poles, this painting represents an endless rhythm and energy. Art scholars have praised a sense of order amidst the chaos of this painting. It has also been used to prove that Pollock's works contain non-random fractal geometry patterns.

Mural on Indian Red Ground - Oil (1950)

Acquired by the queen Farah Pahlavi, an avid Iranian collector who established the Tehran Museum of Contemporary Art in 1977, however the painting was exhibited for the first time in 2005 long after the 1979 revolution.

Lavender Mist No. 1 - Oil and Enamel (1950)

A vast and alive expanse of colored scribbles that the eye naturally follows, that marks the end of the color drip painting era for Pollock.

No. 32 - Oil (1950)

Pollock stops using color, to spread the paint over such a large canvas Pollock had to run across it creating very expressive lines and shapes

Elegant Lady No. 5 - Enamel (1951)

Part of a series of black enamel paintings executed in 1951, he experimented with syringes to apply the enamel, there is also a partial return to figuration.

In Echo No. 25 - Enamel (1951)

Another black enamel painting from the same period as No. 5, it has some figuration elements while remaining abstract.

Convergence - Oil (1952)

This painting is an outsider, an unexpected but brief return to his color drip painting method. It was well received in the cold war representing American freedom of speech, it was even promoted by the CIA since it stayed away from communist social realism and political gestures.

Ocean Greyness - Oil (1953)

Several disembodied eyes can be seen hidden within the swirling colored fragments, Pollock tries to reintroduce the quasi-figurative elements from the start of his career.

The Deep - Oil and Enamel (1953)

An extreme simplification of color and content, its symbolism and title have prompted considerable debate over the years.

Painting is a state of being. Painting is self-discovery. Every good artist paints what he is.
