Contributor Covenant

programminggithuboopensource

Many Open Source projects need a Code of Conduct to protect the freedom and equity needed in these virtual environments. Knowing the need for this kind of norms where a collective coding experience takes place, Coraline Ada Ehmke has created a standard Code of Conduct.

This project hopes to increase the diversity of contributors, the number of women, people of color, and other vulnerable populations, so they can freely contribute with new ideas and points of view. The main problem is that many open source projects originate in meritocratic communities, which end up alienating some people and discriminating others by assuming that everyone has the same amount of resources, time and personal experiences.

Coraline, in addition to creating the code of conduct, has contributed to countless open source projects. Caroline's views of inclusion and diversity are expressed in detail within three posts she made for Model View Culture magazine:

  1. Codes of Conduct 2014.
  2. Meritocracy.
  3. Codes of Conduct 2015.

The software industry has developed a meritocratic system that appears to be inclusive, yet it is nothing more than another power system in urgent need of diversification if it hopes to avoid the normalization of its current discriminatory practices. In other words in a meritocratic system great intellectual contributors are justified by their position of power and influence to discriminate other members of their community, this is a common dehumanizing and utilitarian philosophy.

Aside from its work on ethical coding and the contributor covenant Caroline has participated in many interesting conferences:

  1. Artisans and Apprentices 2014: Compares differences between current educational and job opportunities with those a millennium ago.
  2. Alchemy and Identity of Programmers 2014: The beginnings of Alchemy where analogous to the recent practice of software development, programmers must be vigilant in an industry where the rules change day by day.
  3. Business Intelligence 2014: How to use Ruby to generate extremely simple and fast database analysis or Business Intelligence.
  4. Code Aesthetics 2014: There is a real relationship between code aesthetics and its usefulness, we need agile, simple, readable and practical code bases. Backup 2015.
  5. Data-Driven Refactoring 2015: How to refactor without breaking the code base in Ruby.
  6. He no longer works here 2015: Exposes his experience as a transgender woman.
  7. Your First Legacy Codebase 2016: How to work with a legacy codebase when you only have experience with new apps.
  8. NEO4J Graphical Database 2016: How to use graphical databases as an alternative to tabular databases.
  9. Metaphors and Similes 2017: Another comparation of alchemy and programming. Software development will be better by taking ideas from other fields to innovate.
  10. Alice Adventures in AI Land 2017: Building process and examples of a bot with good language capabilities.
  11. Open Source Inequality 2018: Every open source proyect and language (including Ruby and PHP) need an enforceable code of conduct to stop discrimination.
  12. Pride and Prejudice 2019: How writing a codebase is similar to writing a good novel, with the same complexities and creativity needed.
  13. Thomas Edison vs Nicola Tesla 2019: How to balance your work as a software developer to include new technology into your workflow without disrupting your current projects.
  14. Empathy in State Machines 2019: What empathy means in an abstraction of a program or state machine.
  15. Rising Storm of Ethics in OS 2021: The role and ethics of open source in the current political and social changes. Backup 2021.
